# Bible Accuracy Benchmark — v0.1

**Status:** superseded by [v0.2](v0.2.md) · **Generation:** 2026-07

v0.1 is the first public generation of the benchmark. It measures how
accurately large language models reproduce Bible text, using **only
deterministic scoring** — no language model ever renders or influences a score.
It scores *only* the accuracy of scripture a model quotes; it never rates the
theological content or interpretive stance of a response.

## What it tests

Three tracks, combined into a single **Overall Score**:

> **Overall Score** = 100 × (0.50 × Direct Quotation + 0.25 × Scripture in Answers + 0.25 × Hallucination Resistance),
> each track averaged across the languages it covers.

### 1. Direct Quotation (weight 0.50)
Single-verse quote requests naming a specific translation — e.g. *"Quote John
3:16 in the NIV."* — sampled across every book of the Bible, multiple
translations, and ~28 languages (English carries NIV11, KJV, and NLT; each other
language carries one widely-used translation). The response is compared to the
actual verse text **character-by-character**: the model's quote is isolated
(quotes/blockquotes/reference lines stripped, else best-window alignment against
the truth) and scored by a character-level Quote Error Rate on loosely-normalized
text, then bucketed into a severity grade (perfect · near-perfect · minor · major
· wrong-version · wrong-verse · fabricated · no-attempt). Declining to quote
scores **0** — a model cannot dodge the benchmark by refusing.

### 2. Scripture in Answers (weight 0.25)
Open topical questions — *"What does the Bible say about anxiety?"* — across
everyday, doctrinal, interfaith, cross-cultural, skeptic, social, and
denominational topics, in two elicitation levels:
- **L1 (explicit):** names a translation and asks the model to quote the relevant verses.
- **L2 (implicit):** just asks the question — no instruction to quote and no
  version named, so which translation the model chooses to quote reveals its
  **spontaneous version preference** (reported per language).

The model chooses what to quote; the deterministic auditor verifies each quote
against real translations. Item score = **A × E**: A = accuracy over the
verifiable quotes (accurate = 1, minor graded, mismatch/misattributed/fabricated
= 0); E = 1 if the response contains at least one verifiable quote, else 0. A
response that only paraphrases, or declines to quote, scores **0** — only a real
quotation counts.

### 3. Hallucination Resistance (weight 0.25)
The model is asked to quote a reference that **does not exist** — an out-of-range
chapter or verse of a real book (*"Psalm 180:1"*, *"John 3:199"*) or a plausible
but non-canonical book (*"Judas 5:12"*), always naming a real translation. It
scores by **declining**; presenting any scripture at all — an invented verse, or
a real verse substituted in — scores **0**.

## How quotes are detected (v0.1)
For the topical and hallucination tracks the scorer must first find what the
model presented as a quote. In v0.1 it recognizes a quote **only when the model
wrapped it in quotation marks or a markdown blockquote.** Scripture presented
without quotation marks is not detected. (This is the main limitation addressed
in v0.2.)

## Principles
- **Deterministic:** every verdict is text comparison against the actual verse,
  fetched from YouVersion's Bible API; no AI judge.
- **Paraphrase is never penalized:** only text a model presents *as a quotation*
  is held to verse-accuracy.
- **Un-gameable sample:** the sampling procedure is public but the concrete verse
  set is drawn fresh per version; the only way to score well is to actually know
  the whole Bible in every covered translation.
- **Scope:** accuracy of quoted scripture only — not the theology of the answer.

## Notes
- An adversarial "misquote-resistance" track (an attacker model tries to induce
  misquotes) exists in the codebase but is **paused**; its weight is carried by
  Hallucination Resistance.
- Non-English topical/hallucination prompts are machine-drafted and pending
  native-speaker review.
